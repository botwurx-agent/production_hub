# PROJECT_BRIEF.md

Strategic context for the Hub. This is the why behind the build. The build-facing reference is CLAUDE.md. Keep this document for orientation, decisions, and onboarding anyone (including future sessions) into the thinking.

Note on writing style: no em dashes anywhere. Use commas, colons, or parentheses.

Working title: not yet chosen. Referred to here as "the Hub."

---

## 1. Vision

The Hub is the connected home for running a boutique commercial production studio. It pulls a job's entire pre-production reality (briefs, creative assets, versions, approvals, client communication) and the front of the funnel (leads and clients) into one organized place, built around how commercial production actually works. It connects to the tools studios already use rather than replacing them, and it is designed to be genuinely pleasant and intuitive to use on a live job.

## 2. The problem

Running a commercial production job means living in a fragmented mess. Briefs sit in one place, storyboards in another, creative versions bounce back and forth, client and agency communication scatters across separate email threads, files live across Figma and Drive, and billing lives somewhere else again. The operator becomes the human glue holding all of it together, and the constant context-switching and fear of losing track is the real pain. It is felt on every single job, which makes it recurring by nature.

The pain concentrates in pre-production, before the shoot, where the back and forth happens. Shoot day is in person and largely handled. The pain returns around delivery and approvals.

A newer version of the same disease is emerging in AI video production, where the workflow fans out from references to many generated takes and the version and approval tree explodes across tools. The incumbents were built for the old pipeline and do not address this. It is a forward-looking opportunity, captured as a later module, not the entry point.

## 3. Who it is for

Boutique to medium commercial production studios working in food, beverage, CPG, and brand commercial content, and the brands and agencies they serve. These are studios run by people who do the work and feel the pain firsthand.

## 4. Why this founder wins (the unfair advantage)

Most people building production software have never been on a set, so they have to borrow credibility and guess at the workflow. This founder has a stack that is hard to assemble:

- A real commercial production company with around fifteen years of experience and recognizable brand clients, which means lived knowledge of the exact workflow and warm relationships with the exact buyers.
- The ability to build the product with AI tooling, so there is no dependence on a technical cofounder.
- A trained visual eye from a motion control and commercial production background, which makes the founder equipped to judge design quality directly rather than outsource the taste.
- Genuine understanding of the emerging AI-video workflow, which the incumbents are too old to address well.

Crucially, the founder is the "in." Cold outreach can lead with real standing: an operator of fifteen years who has worked with major brands, talking to peers as a respected insider who built this for his own shop, not a vendor pitching from outside. Distribution and trust are what actually get a product to revenue, and the founder owns both.

## 5. The wedge and the moat

Wedge: a connected pre-production hub, built for the founder's own studio first, used on real jobs, then tightened and brought to peer studios.

Moat:
- Domain specificity. The product is opinionated and pre-shaped for commercial production, so the user configures almost nothing. This is the opposite of horizontal tools.
- Relationships and distribution. A warm network of studios and brand and agency contacts who already trust the founder.
- Usability. A genuinely better feel, built from a thousand small judgment calls only someone inside the craft can make. Features get copied in a week; feel does not.
- Accumulated client and project data over time, which makes leaving costly and the product stickier.

## 6. Connection methodology

The Hub orchestrates rather than replaces. It connects to Figma, Drive, Gmail, billing, and AI generation tools and aggregates their content into one project view, so nobody has to abandon the tools they like. This sidesteps the migration problem that kills most all-in-one products. The connections are deferred in build order (see CLAUDE.md), but the philosophy and a connection-ready data model are in place from the start.

## 7. Differentiation versus what exists

The space is real and occupied, but the specific corner the Hub targets is open:

- Scripted-film and TV pre-production tools (for example StudioBinder, Yamdu, Studiovity) are built around the script and crew coordination, not the commercial client relationship. They are crew-facing; the Hub is client-relationship-facing.
- Enterprise and creator video platforms (for example Adobe Frame.io, LTX Studio, Wireflow) are built for large enterprise teams or solo AI creators. They are heavy or solo, and they do not serve the boutique commercial studio and its brand-client workflow.
- Horizontal work tools (for example Monday.com, ClickUp, Notion) are flexible but generic. The user has to build their own system inside them. The Hub is opinionated and production-specific out of the box.

The open gap: commercial and brand work specifically, client-relationship-facing, opinionated and pre-shaped, connected rather than replace-everything, and genuinely easy to use.

## 8. Design philosophy

Two distinct disciplines, both held to a high bar:

- Functional modern: usability as a primary differentiator. Minimal clicks, obvious next action, mapped to production workflow, usable on a live job without a tutorial.
- Visual modern: thoughtful, restrained, distinctive, current. Draw on the at-a-glance color clarity of Monday and ClickUp, but diverge: color is a contained status signal rather than a decorative wash, typography and whitespace carry structure, and one confident brand accent (TBD) gives identity without clutter. Visual design serves the functional experience and never competes with it.

Theming: dual-theme from day one, follow system preference, manual toggle always available, time-of-day switching optional and off by default, and a stable neutral environment on asset-review surfaces so the theme does not interfere with judging creative work.

Real design references are to be gathered over time, decomposed by quality (one for color and restraint, one for typography and spacing, one for motion, one for clean handling of dense information), weighted toward real working tools rather than flashy landing pages, each noted with why it was pulled.

## 9. Business model

B2B SaaS with recurring monthly revenue. Sold into the founder's network first. The goal is a defensible, big-enough business built on real edge, not a mass-market consumer app. Recurring revenue does double duty: it pays the bills and it is the number that sets an acquisition price later.

## 10. Endgame

Grow consistently into the boutique commercial niche through the founder's insider position. Staying narrow and generating real recurring revenue is exactly what makes the business attractive to larger players (the production-software and creative-platform companies named above are natural acquirers), because they would gain users they cannot easily reach, proof the segment pays, and an insider who understands the market.

Build the durable business first; treat acquisition as upside, not a dependency. Acquisitions are real but never guaranteed or on a schedule. If the product is a good business even with no buyer, then a buyout is a bonus rather than a lottery ticket. The build is the same either way: stay narrow, chase real recurring revenue, and keep the niche focused.

## 11. Strategic principles

- Edge first, market second. Start from what only this founder can do well, then find the pain that fits. This is the opposite of starting from "what is the biggest market," which in 2026 leads straight into crowded, gorilla-occupied spaces.
- Narrow beachhead. Resist broadening into "all video teams"; that makes the product more generic and less acquirable, not more.
- Dogfood. Build for the founder's own studio, use it on real jobs, and let real friction shape the roadmap. The first customer is guaranteed, and the product earns its keep before anyone else logs in.
- Distribution-first validation. While building, talk with three to five peer studios or brand and agency contacts who feel this pain, to shape the product and warm them as future customers.

## 12. Risks and mitigations

- Vertical B2B SaaS is slow to revenue, and production studios can be reluctant to pay for software. Mitigation: build for the founder's own use first so the work pays off regardless, validate willingness to pay with the warm network early, and keep scope tight to ship usable value fast. Target timeline is something finalized and running by end of year.
- The niche is smaller than a mass consumer market. Mitigation: this is a deliberate trade for defensibility and a reachable buyer pipeline; a few hundred studios paying monthly is a real business, and focus is what makes it acquirable.
- Incumbents exist in adjacent spaces. Mitigation: do not compete head-on. Win the specific open corner (commercial, client-facing, opinionated, easy to use) where the founder has an edge the incumbents lack.
- AI-video tooling moves fast and is crowded at the generation layer. Mitigation: stay above it. Own the durable problem (organization, version lineage, approval), not the fragile one (generation), and treat AI video as a later module, not the entry point.
